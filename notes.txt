<div class="hero-tagline">Rūḥā d’Qudshā -the Holy Spirit</div>
<p>Mary transitioned from being a vessel for a "shattered" spirit to becoming the vessel for the one "Whole" Feminine Spirit.</p>
<p>Rūḥā means "Breath" or "Wind" in Hebrew and the Aramaic.  To the people of Magdala, if Jesus  "breathed life back into Mary, a woman who had "caught her breath" after years of gasping under the weight of the "seven wrongs" it would have looked like a miracle.</p>

<p>In the Hebrew and Aramaic tradition, Isaiah 11:2 references the Seven Spirits of God.  They symbolize the complete and perfect work in the lives of believers.</p>

            <p style="margin-left: 25px;"><strong>From Pariah to Patron: </strong>The text says that immediately after being healed, she began to fund the ministry. This suggests that her "demons" had perhaps kept her from her own productivity, wealth, or family resources, which were unlocked once she was healed.</p>
        <p style="margin-left: 25px;">When Jesus tells  Peter to forgive "seventy time seven" in Matthew 18:22, he is invoking <strong>Seven</strong> as the number of "infinite completion".</p>
		<p style="margin-left: 25px;">If 7x70 is the "infinite capacity to forgive", then the <strong>Seven Demons</strong> should be seen as the "infinite capacity to be wounded".</p>
		
		<p style="margin-left: 25px;">If Mary had been "wronged seven times"—perhaps through the loss of family, social shaming in the high-stakes world of Magdala, or systemic abuse—her "possession" was the psychological weight of those unhealed wounds.</p>
